Synopsis

The story starts off with the two once best friends riding in separate trains. There is a monologue about their current life and events that have occurred. They start to reminisce about the past and think about the one fateful winter night where the two of them slowly drifted apart. Suddenly, we see the two boys in their childhood days playing Pakèmon. Dialogue is exchanged and they subtly state their problems at home. They then bring up the mayor statue a top the mountain and the green boy decides he wants to blow it up. Following the green boys idea, the blue boy wants to run away after blowing it up far away from where they live. The two agree to the conditions and make plans to do it the next day.

As they explore through the forest going up the mountain, they come across a dead deer. The two inspect it and walk away from it. Suddenly, they start to feel sick and pass out near the carcass. A monster comes up to the boys, as if inspecting them, and turns away. Upon waking up, the two starts to see different realities, the green boy sees vibrant visuals where people are animals while the blue boy sees more dull colors and people and items appear as machines. They encounter different characters (Spouses, a hunter, a dog, and Haru) as they traverse through the forest and we learn more about the boys personalities and their viewpoints. While traversing the mountain, they come across a person who looks normal, not a machine or animal like the many others. All three stay the night in a cave by a campfire and talk about friendship and the real reason the blue kid wants to run away.

They wake up and notice the boy Haru is gone. They keep traversing the mountain trying to find Haru and to reach the top of the mountain. The two boys reached the statue and get ready to blow up the statue with the fireworks. Turns out the fireworks they brought couldn’t blow up the statue. After not being able to blow up the statue, the two laugh about the firework and find revelation about their journey. They pass out again for a quick second and wake up. Once they come to, they see the world as normal. The blue kid changed his mind on running away from home and the green boy just followed him to keep him safe.

A scream is heard nearby so the boys go and inspect it. They suddenly come across the same creature that inspected them when passed out and see it swallow a woman. They have a life or death situation where one has to live and the other dies. The one who left comes back to save their best friend and drive the monster away while saving the woman who was swallowed. We find ourselves back on the train we started and get the epilogue of what happened upon returning from the mountain. The two drifted apart after some time occurred post running away and the story ends there.

Gameplay

BOKURA is described as a two-player co-op puzzle platformer. The players go through puzzles as children and then transition into different realities: one sees animals, the other sees machines. Players must communicate effectively as they traverse through the forest/mountain, each being able to interact with different objects. Big time decisions must be made which influence later events along the game.

The soundtrack is the same when the boys are seen in human form. Once the players are thrown in the different perspectives, the soundtrack and sound effects change depending what one sees. The green side has vibrant colors, so there is a more upbeat soundtrack for what he sees. The blue side has a more lax soundtrack with its calming and robust visuals. An example is in a puzzle there is a "dog". This dog chases only the green boy while the blue boy sees the dog a s machine and can step on it. When the blue kid steps on top of the dog, it creates a machine noise while the green kid hears the bones coming out of the dog.

The story is beautifully made and simplistic. We learn more about each kid’s personality and traits as we progress through the game. There are certain moments in the story where players see different dialogue and must converse on which decision is best to make. What I barely found out is it can be played three times for three different endings to be seen. The only part that remains constant is the puzzles you go through. It is a neat and nice detail that encourages players to experience the game multiple times.

Characters

Blue Kid (Age 10): Lives with his mother, who often goes to the bar with her boyfriend. His father is not present in the picture and does not have much money. His worldview turns to machinery and dull.

Green Kid (Age 10): Comes from a wealthy family, but is ignored by both of the parents. He feels invisible by his parents and argue frequently. His worldview turns to vivid and animals.

Overall

I find the game interesting because of the gameplay aspects and the narrative. I am always a sucker for soundtracks so I found it fascinating that each perspective has its own soundtrack and sound effects. It may not have 2d smooth visuals, but the eight-bit style gives the game some life as well. Some puzzles were easier than others and I how the two players have their own things to interact with. It made me feel like both players were equal.

When it comes to the narrative, I like how different but alike the two boys are. We know no background on the boys when we first begin. As we progress the story and encounter other characters, we learn more about the boys. Their interactions with the other characters reveal deeper layers of their personalities, such as the campfire dialogue with Haru and with the Father and Mother searching for their kid. Upon inspecting the picture above, we see the way the two kids see their worlds. One steam user notes the way the kids see their different reality is how the kids feels on the inside and their experiences in the real world.

Connection to Readings

Lacan's Mirror Stage

We learned about Lacans's mirror stage and then had to reflect it to the game of LIMBO. The professor states "Our selves are not isolated, uniform static beings; they are in a constant dialectic exchange with our own reflections in the mirror". I relate this reading to the game because there is some kind of attachment we feel for the characters we play as. Both characters have their own personalities and problems at home. We can relate this to ourselves by our experiences, or another way to put it, looking into a mirror. With this in mind, I connect Silvermans idea of the suture to the game as well.

Silverman and Suture

Suture was described as "something quite vivid and specific that connects you to the game". There are a few specifics in the game that make one feel connected to the game. The two boys mention "Pakemon" on the train and we travel back in time when the two boys were kids playing the game. I would say many grew up playing or knowing the famous game "Pokemon" so this connects the players and reminds them of their childhood days with that specific friend. For instance, I had a childhood best friend who i would always play Pokemon with. As we grew up, we kind of seperated and do not speak to each other often. Then the other part of the game is the blue kid wanting to run away and the green kid wanting to cause mischeif by blowing up the statue. The way the two characters are and their familyu history is, players can feel connected to the two young kids.
